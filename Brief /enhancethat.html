The model
Design → Sell → Make

The fashion industry has run the same model for 50 years. Design. Make. Then hope it sells. The brands gaining ground today have reversed it. They test demand before they commit. They make what they've already sold.

Getting there isn't a strategy problem. It's a workflow and systems problem — and that's what this page is about.

01 — The problem

The current model bets everything before the market has spoken.

Traditional fashion works like this: spend months designing a collection, commit to bulk production, then put it in front of customers and hope. Inventory builds. Discounting follows. Margin takes the hit.

It's not that brands don't know this is broken. It's that changing the model requires changing how everything works — the tools, the processes, the handoffs between teams and suppliers.

02 — What it means

Design → Sell → Make in practice.

The idea sounds simple. The execution isn't. Selling before you make means showing product that doesn't exist yet — which requires digital assets good enough to drive real decisions. It means collecting and reading demand signals in ways most brands aren't set up to do. And it means having a supply chain that can move fast enough to fulfil when demand is confirmed.

Each stage depends on the previous one working. And each stage requires its own workflow and system infrastructure.

Design stage — old waySketch and sample. Physical prototypes. Long approval loops before anything reaches an audience.
Design stage — new wayDigital-first. 3D assets, product intelligence, structured data that moves downstream without being re-entered.
Sell stage — old wayBulk order placed. Inventory committed. Sales are the outcome of production, not the input to it.
Sell stage — new wayTest demand before committing. Drops, campaigns, pre-orders. Real signals from real audiences inform what gets made.
Make stage — old wayProduction brief assembled manually. Supplier communication by email. Long lead times baked in.
Make stage — new wayDigital order flows directly into production. Suppliers receive structured data. Scale what worked, move on from what didn't.
03 — Why it's hard

Most brands understand the model. Very few can execute it.

The gap isn't vision. It's infrastructure. Three things stop most brands from getting there:

01

Product data isn't structured

Design intent lives in the heads of designers and scattered across files. Without structured product data, nothing can flow automatically downstream.

02

Tools aren't connected

The design tool doesn't talk to the PLM. The PLM doesn't talk to the supplier portal. Every handoff is a manual step — and a potential error.

03

Workflows were built for the old model

The existing process — and the people running it — are optimised for Design → Make → Sell. Changing requires rebuilding processes, not just buying new software.
